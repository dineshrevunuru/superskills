# Conduct User Interview

Execute a live interview session so the participant does 90% of the talking and every answer traces to real, recalled experience — not polite speculation.

## When to use / when NOT to use

**Use when:**
- Running a discovery/attitudinal interview session (remote or in person) — from opening consent to closing
- Prepping to facilitate: probe repertoire, silence handling, observer briefing
- Recovering mid-session: terse participant, rambling participant, observer interrupting, you caught yourself leading

**NOT for — go to the sibling skill:**
- Writing or ordering the questions themselves → `write-interview-guide` (owns the 7-step guide process, coverage matrix, leading/double-barreled lint)
- Watching someone attempt tasks on a product → `moderate-usability-session` (interviews capture what people SAY; usability tests capture what people DO)
- Interviewing business stakeholders about goals/constraints → `conduct-stakeholder-interviews`
- Observing people in their real environment → `run-contextual-research`
- What to do with the notes afterward → `synthesize-research-data`

## The method — session arc

### 0. Pre-session (day before + 15 min before)

- [ ] Guide piloted at least once (owned by `write-interview-guide` — confirm it happened; if not, dry-run on a colleague now)
- [ ] Recording tool tested; backup recording method ready (phone, second device)
- [ ] Consent script printed/pinned — recording, data use, right to skip or stop
- [ ] Notes template open: two columns — **Observation** (verbatim quotes, timestamps) | **Interpretation** (your hunches, marked as yours)
- [ ] Observers briefed (see Observer management below) BEFORE the participant joins
- [ ] Your own state: single task. Facilitating IS the job — no Slack, no note-perfectionism. If budget allows, a dedicated notetaker; otherwise record and take sparse timestamp notes.

### 1. Opening (2–3 min)

Say, in your own words:
1. Who you are and the session length.
2. "I'm here to learn from your experience — there are no right or wrong answers, and nothing you say can disappoint me."
3. "You can skip any question or stop at any time."
4. "May I record this? The recording is only for my notes and is [deleted/kept] per [policy]." — **wait for an explicit yes before recording.**
5. "Any questions before we start?"

### 2. Rapport warmup (3–5 min)

- Open with easy, factual, self-descriptive questions ("What does a typical Tuesday look like for you?"). Never open with the hardest research question.
- Mirror lightly: match their pace and energy, nod, keep eye contact (camera lens on remote calls).
- Do not interrupt. Ever. Let every sentence land fully before you speak.
- Rapport is not chit-chat minutes — it's the participant learning, from your behavior, that long answers are welcome.

### 3. The funnel — broad to narrow

Run each topic in the guide as its own funnel:

1. **Grand tour** (broadest): "Walk me through the last time you [did the activity]."
2. **Critical incident** (anchor in a real memory): "Tell me about a specific time when [X] went badly / surprisingly well." Real recalled events beat generalities every time.
3. **Narrow follow-ups**: only after the broad layers, ask about the specific details the research questions need.
4. **Never lead the funnel downward with your own vocabulary or hypothesis.** Use THEIR words for THEIR things.

Phrasing law: open questions start with **how / what / tell me about**. Questions starting with **did / was / is / do you** are closed and often leading — save them only for factual confirmation ("Was that on your phone or laptop?").

### 4. Probing — the repertoire

Probe MORE than feels polite. Insufficient probing is a named NN/g facilitation mistake; the surface answer is almost never the finding.

| Probe | How it works | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Echo** | Repeat their last key phrase with rising intonation: "…it felt like a chore?" | They said something loaded and stopped. Cheapest probe; invisible. |
| **Boomerang** | Return their question as a question: "What should this button do?" → "What would you expect it to do?" | They ask YOUR opinion or how something works. Never answer mid-session. |
| **Columbo** | Play naive, trail off, let them finish: "So you ended up calling instead, because…?" | You suspect a reason but must not supply it. Half-sentences pull complete stories. |
| **Neutral expand** | "Tell me more about that." / "What do you mean by [their word]?" / "What happened next?" | Any answer that's shorter than the experience it describes. |
| **Significance** | "Why is that important to you?" | They state a preference or ritual without stakes attached. |
| **Silence** | Say nothing. Count 5–10 seconds. Attentive face. | ALWAYS try this first after an answer. People fill silence with the good stuff. |

**Silence rule:** the discomfort you feel at second 4 is yours, not theirs. If you jump in early you buy their headline and lose the story. Wait the full count; only then probe verbally.

**Probe decision tree:**
- Answer feels complete and concrete → silence anyway (once) → move on.
- Answer is abstract/general ("usually", "people tend to") → critical incident: "Tell me about the last specific time."
- Answer contradicts something earlier → Columbo, never confrontation: "Earlier you mentioned [X], and just now [Y] — help me put those together…"
- Answer is a question to you → boomerang.
- Answer is emotional but unexplained → echo the emotion word.

### 5. Live mistake recovery

| You notice | Fix, in the moment |
|---|---|
| You just asked a leading question ("Don't you find that annoying?") | Don't apologize at length. Re-ask neutrally: "Let me ask that better — how did that feel?" Mark the tainted answer in notes. |
| You rephrased their observation in YOUR words and they agreed | Their agreement is worthless (acquiescence). Re-elicit: "Say it back to me in your own words?" |
| Participant is terse (one-word answers) | You narrowed too fast. Climb back UP the funnel to a grand-tour question; re-warm rapport; slow your own pace. |
| Participant rambles off-topic | "That's really helpful — I want to make sure we have time for [topic]. Earlier you said [bridge]…" Redirect via THEIR earlier words, not your agenda. |
| Participant asks "am I giving you what you need?" | "You're doing exactly right — there's no wrong answer here." Then continue; do not evaluate specific answers. |
| You're behind on time | Cut whole topics (lowest-priority per the guide), never compress probing on the topics you keep. Ten shallow answers < four deep ones. |

### 6. Observer management

Poorly managed observers are a named NN/g facilitation mistake — they can wreck rapport in one interjection.

- **Before the session:** brief observers — silent during; cameras off/muted on remote; questions go to a back channel (chat/doc) and YOU ask them at the end, rewritten neutrally.
- **Introduce honestly, minimally:** "A colleague is joining to take notes." Never a wall of silent faces — cap visible observers at 1–2; the rest watch the stream/recording.
- **If an observer jumps in with a leading question:** absorb and repair — "Building on that: how do YOU think about [topic]?" Debrief the observer after, not in front of the participant.
- **Observer questions at the end:** filter them. Rewrite leading ones; drop out-of-scope ones. You own the session's neutrality.

### 7. Closing (5 min)

1. "Is there anything I should have asked you about, but didn't?" — reliably yields a top-3 insight.
2. Check the back channel for observer questions; ask the filtered ones.
3. Thank them, state what happens next, deliver/confirm the incentive.
4. Stop recording ONLY after asking "anything else?" — post-"official-end" comments are often the most honest; if they say something valuable after recording stops, ask permission to note it.

### 8. Debrief (within 30 minutes, non-negotiable)

Memory decays in hours. Before opening anything else, write the debrief (format below).

## The say-vs-do caveat (interpretation law)

Interviews are **attitudinal self-report**. People misremember, rationalize, and perform social desirability — not from dishonesty, but because memory works that way.

- An interview answer is evidence of **what the participant believes and how they frame it** — never proof of what they actually do.
- Any claim shaped like "users do X" or "users will do X" sourced from interviews alone is **under-evidenced by definition** — handle it per `craft-critique`'s evidence-discipline protocol. The data that strengthens it is behavioral (logs, observation, usability sessions), never more interviews.
- If the real question is "can people use this?" or "what do people actually do?" — an interview is the wrong instrument. Say so and route to `moderate-usability-session` or `run-contextual-research`.
- Prediction questions ("Would you pay for this?") are banned. People cannot predict their own behavior. Ask about past behavior instead.

## Worked example — annotated exchange

Context: discovery interview about how salon clients book appointments (guide already written via `write-interview-guide`).

> **F:** Walk me through the last time you booked an appointment at a salon. *(grand tour — broad, anchored in a real event)*
> **P:** Um, I usually just call.
> **F:** *(silence, 6 seconds)* *(resists "why not online?" — leading + narrowed too fast)*
> **P:** …I mean, I tried their website once but I couldn't tell if the person I like was even working that day, so calling is just safer.
> **F:** Safer? *(echo — one word, their word)*
> **P:** Yeah — last time I booked online I got someone new and the cut was wrong, and I had a wedding that weekend. Never again.
> **F:** Tell me about that time — what happened after? *(critical incident + neutral expand)*
> **P:** I called and they fixed it, but I had to take another morning off work…
> **F (later):** So now, before every booking, you… *(Columbo — trails off; does NOT supply "call to check first")*
> **P:** I text the receptionist first. She tells me when Maria's in. THEN I book.

What the funnel bought: the headline was "I usually just call." The finding — a trust-and-recovery workaround routed through a specific stylist and a back-channel text — only surfaced through silence → echo → incident → Columbo. Notes record: *Observation:* "Never again" + wedding story (quote, 00:14:20). *Interpretation (mine):* stylist continuity may outweigh booking convenience — check against other participants. And per the say-vs-do caveat: this is her account of what she does; if booking-flow redesign hangs on it, corroborate with booking logs or observation.

## Anti-patterns / red flags

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| Fill silence at second 3 | Count to 8. Their next sentence is the finding. |
| "Don't you think X is frustrating?" | "How did that feel?" — they name the emotion. |
| Answer "how does this work?" mid-session | Boomerang: "What would you expect?" |
| Rephrase their point in your words for confirmation | Ask them to say it in their words. Their nod to YOUR phrasing is noise. |
| Accept "usually I…" generalities | "Tell me about the last specific time." |
| "Would you use/pay for this?" | "When did you last [related past behavior]? Walk me through it." |
| Take verbatim-everything notes while facilitating | Record + sparse timestamped notes; full attention on the person. |
| Let a stakeholder interject live | Back channel, filtered by you, asked at the end. |
| Treat interview quotes as behavioral proof in the readout | Label self-report as self-report; triangulate per `craft-critique`. |
| Skip the debrief because the recording exists | Write the debrief within 30 min — recall, not transcripts, holds the connections. |

## Output format — post-session debrief

```markdown
## Debrief — P[n], [date, session #]
**Top 3 takeaways:** (one line each — what you'd tell the team in the hallway)
**Surprises / contradictions:** (incl. says-vs-earlier-says tensions to test in later sessions)
**Verbatim quotes worth keeping:** (with timestamps)
**Facilitation notes:** (questions that flopped, leading slips → feed back to write-interview-guide)
**Guide changes before next session:** (specific edits, or "none")
**Coverage:** research questions touched / still starving
```

## Boundaries

- `write-interview-guide` owns question creation, funnel ORDERING on paper, coverage matrix, and bias lint. This skill owns live delivery and adaptation of that guide.
- `moderate-usability-session` owns task-based sessions (think-aloud, no premature rescue). If the participant is USING a product during the session, that skill governs.
- `conduct-stakeholder-interviews` owns business-goal interviews and power-interest context.
- `run-contextual-research` owns interviews conducted in the user's environment interleaved with observation.
- `synthesize-research-data` owns everything after the debrief (coding, themes, need statements).
- `craft-critique` is the single source of the evidence-discipline protocol — this skill applies it to self-report claims, never restates it.
